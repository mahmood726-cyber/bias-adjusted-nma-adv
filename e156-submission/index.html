1. Scoped Down-Weighting

Within-study contrast covariance matrices ($V_s$) are scaled by their quality weights ($w_s \in (0, 1]$) representing risk of bias (RoB):

V_{s,\text{weighted}} = V_s / w_s

This inflates the variances of high-bias/low-quality studies, naturally reducing their GLS weights without hard exclusions.

2. Context Meta-Regression

Covariates modulate both treatment effects and design bias terms via linear interaction design matrices:

y = X_{\text{trt}} \beta + X_{\text{bias}} \delta + (X_{\text{trt}} \cdot C) \gamma_{\text{trt}} + (X_{\text{bias}} \cdot C) \gamma_{\text{bias}} + \epsilon

This allows design bias (NRS vs RCT) and treatment comparisons to vary continuously with study context (e.g. publication year, baseline risk).

3. Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman (HKSJ) Scaling

The standard error covariance matrix $\Sigma_0 = (X^T M^{-1} X + P)^{-1}$ is adjusted by the residual weighted sum of squares factor $q$:

q = \max\left(1.0, \frac{(y - X \hat{\beta})^T M^{-1} (y - X \hat{\beta})}{df}\right)
\Sigma_{\text{adjusted}} = q \cdot \Sigma_0

Confidence intervals are computed using the Student-t distribution with $df = n_{\text{studies}} - p$ degrees of freedom instead of normal approximations.

4. Continuous vs Binary Outcome Agnosticism

Our estimator processes both data types by mapping them to contrast vectors and blocked covariance matrices:

  • Binary: Estimates log-odds ratios using empirical cell counts: $y_{ik} = \ln(\text{odds}_k) - \ln(\text{odds}_i)$ with corresponding variance blocks $V_i$.
  • Continuous: Directly accepts mean differences $y_{ik} = \mu_k - \mu_i$ and propagates standard error squares $V_i = \text{SE}_k^2 + \text{SE}_i^2$.

Once contrast vectors and covariance blocks are computed, the downstream GLS fitting engine behaves identically, preserving mathematical properties across both endpoints.

5. RCT-Only Adjustments (ClinicalTrials.gov)

When pooling exclusively RCT evidence (e.g. from ClinicalTrials.gov), design stratification (RCT vs NRS) is irrelevant. However, study-specific biases still manifest due to individual trial risk-of-bias. We formulate a study-specific coupled bias prior:

\sigma^2_{\text{bias}, s} = \sigma^2_{\text{bias\_base}} \cdot (1 - w_s)

Where $w_s \in (0, 1]$ represents the trial quality score. Low-risk/perfect RCTs ($w_s = 1.0$) force the study bias to 0, while high-risk/flawed RCTs ($w_s < 1.0$) allow the estimator to fit and subtract a study-specific bias term, adjusting the pooled treatment contrast toward the true unconfounded effect.
